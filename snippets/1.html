How the Street

Changed

New York

an urban spatial analysis about hip-hop in 90s

words and coding by Andrea Nasuto

Before the hypebeast(ed) Supreme, before Louis Vuitton became the new  design playground for Virgil Abloh, before Kendrick Lamar won the Pulitzer, hip hop was deep-rooted in the life from the ghettos. It told their stories as no other of contemporary music has ever captured. New York hip hop in 90s stays attached to their reality. A reality told by young males in their 20s living in inner cities with no hope of getting out of them. A reality made by single mothers unable to feed their kids because in crack overdose, extraordinary incarceration rates and teenagers selling cocaine.

Role models: Pablo Escobar and Tony Montana.

It was still the American Dream but at its extremes. This is also a stories about talent, stubbornness and happiness.

A story about how inner cities can changed the world.
How some kids shaped the current most listened to genre in the world: hip hop. How some ghettos became the Silicon Valley of music.

In April 1994, a 21 years old African American boy, called Nasir Jones, releases his debut album: 'Illmatic'.

The album name is an expression coming from his neighborhood, Queensbridge, where he is known with an another name: Nas.

Just four months later, another 22 years old black New Yorker, releases his first album, 'Ready To Die'. The title doesn't need explanations. 

He grew up in Clinton Hill, Brooklyn and his name is Cristopher Wallance but neither he is known for his birth name. His name in the 'hood is Big, Notorious B.I.G.

The same year, Jeru The Damaja and Method Man came out with their debut album. In November 1993, Wu Tang had their first album published and A Tribe Called Quest completed is masterpiece "Midnight Marauders".

In less than 10 months, hip hop was changed by these young men as it would have probably never been changed in a such short period of time in the history of the music. They all had almost the same age and they were all from New York. 

How this could happen?

In 1992, the city experienced the highest unemployment rate in 40 years, crack epidemic was still going on and the number of murders was 574% higher than nowadays. Even jogging in Central Park was not safeIn the 90s New York City was far from being the glossy gentrified capital of the world captured in 'Devil Wears Prada'.

"I always say my environment wrote that album.

New York wrote that album — Queensbridge, specifically."

Nas

We ain't doin crime for the sake of doing crimes/
We movin dimes cause we ain't doin fine.


Jay-Z - "Can I Live", 1996

 

 

Jay-z grew up in one of the toughest neighborhood in New York, Marcy Housing Projects where unemployment was 25.9% and residents with college education were less than 1.5%. Unemployment rate was generally high but in the blocks were these rappers lived, unemployment was 15.5% on average. The most cronic situation was in the projects where it was almost 3 times higher than citywide average. Iraq in 2014 had a lower unemployment rate compared to New York Public Housing, according to estimates by World Bank. After dropping out of high school, a teen Jay-Z became a drug dealer as other young African-American in those years. The diffuse criminalty together with new efforts to bring down crime rate, led to an unprecedent incarceration rate among black male population both in New York City and United States. In 1993, the number of African-American males incarcerated in US was more than 7 times higher than white citizens.

These new policies drastically reduced crime rate in New York, giving a new life to five boroughs. In the areas selected, crime rate decreased by 79%.

At the end of these changes, a new urban phenomenon started: gentrification.

It drastically changed some of these neighborhoods both in their demographics and in their architecture.
The house where Notorious B.I.G grew up was listed 3 years ago for more than $700,000. The price is almost 200% higher than average value of the houses in the same block in 1990 (adjusted for inflation).

The 'projects' stand for 'New York Public Housing Projects', the largest public housing initiative in North America largely developed by Robert Moses and inspired by Le Corbusier ideas.
They were home of: Jay-Z, Mos Def, Puff Daddy, Fat Joe, Wu-Tang and many others. Easily recognizable for their brown brick building exteriors, they are an example of a 'copy-paste' architecture spreaded out in the 5 boroughs. Facade, interiors, residential concept, years of construction, parks (even lights!), they are basically all the same. They are mentioned endless times in the artists lyrics as places of economic struggles and violence. They also appear in many of music videos produced during those years. Hip hop itself was born in the projects in 80s, specifically in the Bronx River Houses, the home of Universal Zulu Nation.

Prof. Alex Pentland from MIT is one of the most cited authors in computer science and one of the greatest data scientists in the world.
His researches based on big data analysis showed that there are two driving forces in boosting creative output in a urban area: the process of discovering new ideas and the process of letting these ideas becoming daily behaviors, defined in other terms with the word 'engagement'.
At street level, this happens when you have restricted areas with daily social tights typical of a village size, coexisting with an easy access to ideas coming outside this small social network. Proximity still plays a fundamental role and the most famous example of this mechanism is Silicon Valley. In other words: you need both a closed tiny lab and worldwide influx of new ideas to boost creativity.
This theory partially explains how a closed claustrophobic ghetto could have a functional role in shaping these hip hop talents. Their marginalization was somehow able to create unique communities creating a sort of closed lab.
It seems that these were not isolated cases.
This was an ecosystem with patterns.

 

I was in the mecca.

I was inside the place that everybody wanted to be.

 

Nas - from "Time is Illmatic", 2014

 

 

Dj Premier, the producer behind the major hip-hop artists in 90s, used to live three block down Biggie's house in Bed-Stuy where also Jay-Z, Mos Def, GZA, Junior Mafia lived. Same urban spatial pattern with Wu-Tang Clan in Staten Island. Jay-Z, Busta Ryhmes, Notorious B.I.G and DFX they went to the same high school during the same years (Jay-Z and Busta Rhymes they even had a rap battle). The same for Cam'ron and Mace in East Harlem. Queensbridge, as already mentioned, is probably the best example of concentration of hip-hop musicians per square mile.
All these rappers used to spend a lot of time in a small community with other peers since the early ages, freestyling, recording tapes and influencing each other. Surprisingly, this behavior was facilitated by the urban structure. The pattern that you see in their stories is a strong intertwined social interaction happening at a street level. At the same time, living in a problematic but still vibrant New York in 90s, helped to develop cross collaborations or using a Silicon Valley language, to develop a cross pollination. A high density urban area (as all the projects) coexists with diverse influences coming from outside.

 

Whether right or wrong/
Streets gave me all I write in the song.


Jay-Z - "Blueprint", 2001

 

 

Two patterns are visible: one is a semi-closed structure where behaviors are fostered in a set environment, the other is a network of external links. In other words, knowing everyone in your neighborhood like in a small village and spending the majority of time in it, had huge impact on building their artistic character. At the same time, the influx of different styles from other neighborhoods was crucial. While the Wu-Tang is hardcore hip hop, Nas is 'the Bob Dylan of hip hop' and Brooklyn rap is the 'go-platinum' hip hop with larger-then-life figures as B.I.G and later on Jay-Z. They all had different styles across the 5 boroughs and this helped the whole New York hip hop to flourish.
Nowadays, no other area in the world has ever produced more 'platinum' hip hop album than New York.
On the flipside, the same closed urban and social mechanism led many of these young African American to foster other behaviors as crimes, use of drugs or early school dropouts (Nas, B.I.G and Jay-Z have been through all of them). Once again, Prof. Pentland’s researches confirm this hypothesis even when it comes to crime rate pattern.

Rap during 90s is a cinematic portrait how the streets were, told through the lenses of young New Yorkers. It is surely no coincidence that it has been defined the "Black CNN" of those years and it had important role in developing African American identity. Their tracks could appear as daily logbooks about local street life and yet they have an incredible ability to transcend boundaries.

An extraordinary example of how 'local' can be 'global'.
There is something linear in it. This music started in the streets of inner-cities by young people belonging to minority groups as African American or Hispanic. Many of their protagonists were sons of immigrants (Notorious B.I.G) or immigrants themselves (DJ Kool Herc, the Bronx creator of hip hop) and yet it changed the world.

 

 

Hip hop cultures may in fact be one of the most important sources for the study of globalization.


Prof. Samy Alim - Stanford University

 

 

They were the others, quoting Kapuscinski. The underdogs, the ones marginalized. No wonder, rap is the current soundtrack of revolutions.
We live in times where the fear of the others is part of our subconscious or in many cases of political agendas. A constant development of mental and physical boundaries. Hip hop can not only suggest the answer of what we are leaving behind those walls but it can display what globalization has built, especially among young people.
This music is about the existence of people who do not appear on the radars.
It is a like testament: 'I am here'.
